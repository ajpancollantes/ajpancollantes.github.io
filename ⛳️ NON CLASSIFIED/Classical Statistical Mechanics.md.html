Classical Statistical Mechanics

When in the XIX century a major problem of theoretical physics was the description of complex systems, with $10^{23}$ degrees of freedom, as required for the foundations of thermodynamics and statistical mechanics, it became clear that new mathematical ideas were needed; one could not reasonably think to consider a Cauchy problem $10^{23}$ initial data. This led to abandon the idea that a physical state is described by a point in phase space and to rather describe a state as a probability measure on the phase space. In this way probability theory and random variables entered in a crucial and philosophically important way into the framework of Physics, at the basis of Classical Statistical Mechanics.
