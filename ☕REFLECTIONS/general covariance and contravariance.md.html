General variance and contravariance

Suppose that a group $G$ acts transitively on a space $X$. A transformation $p\in G$ can be thought of as "moving" the objects in $X$ or as a change of "point of view" in the following way.

Suppose we have a comfortable, mathematically speaking, space with a distinguished point, $(S,s_0)$; and a bijection $\phi:S\to X$ (in the line of reasoning of homogeneous space#Intuitive approach). That is, for every $x\in X$ we have a kind of "coordinates" $s$ for $x$, given by $s=\phi^{-1}(x)\in S$. Think, for example, of a vector space $V$ and the isomorphism $\phi:V\to \mathbb R^2$ (fixing a basis in $V$). We can think of this as our initial point of view, and we can imagina as if we were located at $x_0=\phi(s_0)\in X$ with some devices to take measurements that let us to create $\phi$ .

An element of the group $g\in G$ is a transformation, a movement, of $X$ into itself.

Observe that $g$ induces a new bijection, a new point of view, $\varphi:S\to X$ given by $\varphi=g^{-1}\circ \phi$. On the other hand, observe that any transformation of $S$, $t$, also leads to a new description: $\phi\circ t$. You can think of $t$ as moving myself and my measurement system to a new location, a new observer.

The key point is: the transformation $g$ has an associated transformation $\tilde{g}:S\to S$ given by $\tilde{g}=\phi^{-1}\circ g\circ \phi$, i.e. induced by the bijection $\phi$. It is impossible to distinguish if the new description $\varphi$ is caused by the transformation $g$ on $X$ or by the transformation $\tilde{g}^{-1}$ on $S$:

$$ \varphi=g^{-1}\circ \phi=(\phi \tilde{g}^{-1}\phi^{-1})\circ\phi=\phi\circ \tilde{g}^{-1} $$

That is to say, it is the same thing to move "the world" while I remain fixed than to move myself in the opposite direction while I leave the world fixed.

Example in Linear Algebra

In the context of Linear Algebra and basis and change of basis, consider the vector space $\mathbb{R}^2$ with the standard basis $\mathcal{C}$. Consider also the alternative basis $\mathcal B$ given by $v_1=(-1,2),v_2=(3,4)$. If we have a vector $w$ with coordinates in this new basis $(1,7)_{\mathcal B}$ we can compute its coordinates in $\mathcal C$ by multiplication with the matrix $A=\begin{pmatrix}-1 & 3\\ 2 & 4 \\ \end{pmatrix}$, as it is well known. But we can interpret this in the context above:

  • $X$, the landscape, is $\mathbb R^2$.

  • $S$, me and my devices, is also $\mathbb R^2$.

  • $\phi$ is the identity.

  • The matrix $A$ is a transformation of $S$ into $S$, and it corresponds to $\tilde{g}^{-1}$, since it change the observer (me with the canonical basis $\mathcal C$) to a new observer (me with the basis $\mathcal B$).

  • The vector $w$ has coordinates $(1,7)$ in the new description, and this is reflected by $\varphi^{-1}(w)=(1,7)$. The initial description of $w$ should be $\phi^{-1}(w)$. But $w=\varphi(1,7)$ so

$$ \phi^{-1}(w)=\phi^{-1}(\varphi(1,7))=\phi^{-1}\circ\phi\circ\tilde{g}^{-1}(1,7)= $$ $$ =\begin{pmatrix}-1 & 3\\ 2 & 4 \\ \end{pmatrix}\cdot \begin{pmatrix}1\\7\\ \end{pmatrix} $$

Compared with my pictures above: if I shift my measurement system by -2, and I tell you that the tree is now at position 7, you can recover the position of the tree at the initial description by applying the same shift, 7-2=5.

Behavior of functions

Suppose a function $f:X\to \mathbb R$ (in the pictures above think of the "height" of every object). The description given by $\phi$ let us create a "more comfortable" function $F_1:S\to \mathbb R$ given by $F_1=f\circ \phi$, encoding the same data

$$ \begin{array}{ccc} X &\stackrel{f}{\longrightarrow} & \mathbb R\\ \phi\uparrow & &\\ S&&\\ \end{array} $$

Consider a transformation $g$ of $X$ (think of a shift of +2, por example, in the picture above). The visual effect corresponds to translate the picture two units right with respect to the scale space $S$. What is the new comfortable description of $f$ with the new description of $X$? Well, the new description of $X$ is $\varphi=g^{-1}\circ \phi$, so the new comfortable function is

$$ F_2=f\circ \varphi=f\circ g^{-1} \circ \phi=F_1\circ \phi^{-1}\circ g^{-1} \circ \phi $$

and

$$ F_2=F_1\circ \tilde{g}^{-1} $$

So the new description of $f$ is obtained by a shift of -2 in the space $S$. This correspond to the elemental fact in high school mathematics that to shift the graph of a function $f(x)$ an amount of $a$ units you write $f(x-a)$.

On the other hand, there is also another approach. Observe that

$$ F_2=f\circ \varphi=f\circ g^{-1} \circ \phi$$ $$ =(f\circ g^{-1}) \circ \phi, $$

so $F_2$ is also the "comfortable description" in the first "point of view" of another function, not $f$ but a transformed one: $f\circ g^{-1}$. What is this new function?

Consider the set $C=\{f(x)=0\}$, the zero set of $f$. The transformation $g$ moves the set $C$ to the set $g C$. Since $y\in gC$ if and only if there exists $y'\in C$ such that $y=g(y')$, with $f(y')=0$. Then, $f(g^{-1}(y))=0$, and therefore

$$ gC=\{x\in X: (f\circ g^{-1})=0\} $$

i.e. the transformed function $f\circ g^{-1}$ is just the function such that its zero set is the transformed of the zero set of $f$.
