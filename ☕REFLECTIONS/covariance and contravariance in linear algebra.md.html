Covariance and contravariance in linear algebra

Let $V$ be a vector space, which we will take to have dimension 3 to simplify things. If we have a base $f=(X_1, X_2, X_3)$ and another base $f'=(Y_1, Y_2, Y_3)$ we can consider a linear map that transforms one into the other. Let $A$ be the matrix of that application with respect to base $f$, that is, the columns of $A$ are the coordinates of each $Y_i$ in base $f$.

Abusing the language it can be written that:

$$ f'= f \cdot A \tag{1} $$

where note that $f$ and $f'$ are row matrices made of vectors (it wouldn't make sense).

This could be written as columns (for me more usual) without more than doing the transpose:

$$ (f')^t= A^t \cdot f^t $$

Now, the matrix $A$ is also used to change the coordinates of any vector $v \in V$. Let us denote by $v[f]$ and $v[f']$ respectively the column matrices with the coordinates of $v$ in $f$ and in $f'$. We have always known that the matrix to go from one base to another is made by putting the coordinates of the original base in columns with respect to the new base, and then multiplying column matrices. Thus, our matrix $A$ would serve to pass from base $f'$ to base $f$. Therefore:

$$ v[f]=A\cdot v[f'] $$

and conversely:

$$ v[f']=A^{-1} \cdot v[f] $$

Comparing this expression with $(1)$ we can say that the coordinates of the vectors are transformed in a contravariant way to how the basis vectors themselves do. Be careful, the contravariance implies the transpose of the inverse!

Now we are going to analyze the coordinates of the covectors, that is, of the elements of the dual space $V^*$. Let $w \in V^*$, with coordinates $w[f^*]$ and $w[f'^*]$ in the respective dual bases of $f$ and $f'$ expressed in columns.

Let's remember that the coordinates of $w$ in a dual base is nothing more than applying $w$ to the original base. Then:

$$ w[f'^*]^t=(w(Y_1), w(Y_2), w(Y_3))= (w(f \cdot A_1), w(f \cdot A_2), w(f \cdot A_3 )) $$

where $A_i$ is the columns of $A$. So that

$$ w[f'^*]^t=(w((X_1, X_2, X_3) \cdot A_1), w((X_1, X_2, X_3) \cdot A_2), w((X_1, X_2, X_3) \cdot A_3))=w[f^*]^t \cdot A $$

where from

$$ w[f'^*]=A^t \cdot w[f^*] $$

which, by comparison with the expression $(1)$, justifies saying that the covectors change their coordinates in a covariant manner.
