Where is the geometry in a Klein geometry?

Suppose we have a Klein geometry $M=G/H$, so we have a $H$-principal bundle $\pi: G\to M$. Suppose it is a reductive Klein geometry, so we have an $Ad(H)$-invariant decomposition of the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$. Then, the Maurer-Cartan form $A$ of $G$ can be accordingly decomposed

$$ \mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{h} \oplus \mathfrak{m}, $$

where $\mathfrak{m}$ is a complementary subspace to $\mathfrak{h}$ in $\mathfrak{g}$ that is invariant under the adjoint action of $H$, i.e., $Ad(h)\mathfrak{m} \subseteq \mathfrak{m}$ for all $h \in H$.

Consider the Maurer-Cartan form $A \in \Omega^1(G, \mathfrak{g})$, an $G$-equivariant map that associates to each $g \in G$ and $v \in T_g G$ an element $A_g(v) \in \mathfrak{g}$. In this context, one can decompose $A$ in accordance with the Lie algebra decomposition:

$$ A = A_{\mathfrak{h}} + A_{\mathfrak{m}}, $$

where $A_{\mathfrak{h}} \in \Omega^1(G, \mathfrak{h})$ and $A_{\mathfrak{m}} \in \Omega^1(G, \mathfrak{m})$ are the respective components of the Maurer-Cartan form with respect to the decomposition.

If we fix a particular $\mathfrak{m}$ and a particular linear isomorphism $i: \mathbb R^n\to \mathfrak m$ we have that given $p\in G$ with $\pi(p)=x$ the following map is a linear isomorphism

$$ \varphi_p : \mathbb R^n\stackrel{i}{\to}\mathfrak m\stackrel{(A_{\mathfrak m})_p^{-1}}{\to}\ker((A_{\mathfrak h})_p) \stackrel{d\pi_p}{\to} T_{x}M , $$

since $(A_{\mathfrak m})_p$ is a linear isomorphism.

On the other hand, for $M$ we have the frame bundle $\pi_{FM}: FM \to M$, which is a principal bundle with the group $GL(n)$. Recall that $F_xM=\{b:\mathbb R^n \to T_x M, b \text{ linear isomorphism} \}$.

We can define a map $h: H \to GL(n)$ such that the map:

$$ \Phi: G \to FM,\quad \Phi(p)=\varphi_p $$

is a bundle morphism over $M$ that respects the group actions, i.e.:

1. $\pi_{FM} \circ \Phi = \pi$.

2. $\Phi(pg) = \Phi(p) \cdot h(g)$, whenever $p \in G$ and $g$ is in $H$.

Specifically, set

$$ h(g) = i^{-1} \circ Ad(g) \circ i, $$

where $Ad(g) : \mathfrak{m} \to \mathfrak{m}$ is the adjoint action of $g$. Then, for $v\in \mathbb R^n$

$$ \Phi(pg)(v)=\varphi_{pg}(v)= d\pi_{pg}\circ(A_{\mathfrak m})_{pg}^{-1}\circ i (v). $$

Observe that $((R_{g^{-1}})^* A_{\mathfrak{m}})_{pg} =(A_{\mathfrak{m}})_p\circ (R_{g^{-1}})_*$ by definition of pullback, and by the properties of the Maurer Cartan form $((R_{g^{-1}})^* A_{\mathfrak{m}})_{pg} = Ad(g) \circ (A_{\mathfrak{m}})_{pg}$, so we have

$$ (A_{\mathfrak{m}})_{pg}^{-1}=(R_{g})_* \circ (A_{\mathfrak{m}})_p^{-1}\circ Ad(g). $$

On the other hand, since $G$ is an $H$-principal bundle, $d\pi_{pg}=d\pi_p \circ (R_{g^{-1}})_*$ (see this). So finally we obtain

$$ \Phi(pg)(v)=d\pi_p \circ (A_{\mathfrak{m}})_p^{-1} \circ Ad(g) \circ i(v)=\varphi_p(h(g)(v))=\Phi(p) \cdot h(g) (v). $$

What we have, then, is nothing but a group reduction of $FM$, or equivalently, an H-structure on $M$. Since they are in 1-to-1 relation with sections of $FM/H$, what we have introduced in $M$ is a choice of a preferred basis in every point $x$ in $M$. And the set of preferred basis is, in a sense, a geometry in $M$

Examples:

  • Rn with a connection (differential geometry)

  • Rn with a volume form

  • Rn with an orientation
