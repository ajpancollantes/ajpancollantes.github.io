Cinf-structure obtained from a non-solvable symmetry algebra

If the symmetry algebra of an $m$th-order ODE is a solvable Lie algebra of dimension $m$ then we have a way to integrate the ODE by quadratures. This can be done "forward" (looking for invariants, constructing the reduced equation,...) or "backward" (with 1-forms). This is Lie theory.

But for the "backward procedure" we don't need so much, it is enough with a weaker object, a solvable structure (Basarab-Horwath). In particular, every solvable Lie symmetry algebra of an ODE gives rise to a solvable structure (Ruiz_2014, proposition 8.4). The knowledge of the solvable structure let us integrate by quadratures, because it let us to construct locally exact 1-forms.

But, what if we settle for less? Indeed, there are 1-forms $\omega$ that although not being exact, give rise to Pfaffian systems $\mathcal{S}(\{\omega\})$ which do have first integrals. This first integral can be found, not by a quadrature, but by solving a PDE system, which many times is affordable (not always, unfortunately). So it is possible that we can solve $m$th order equations with an object even weaker than solvable structures. Thus we arrive to the notion of cinf-structure.

Within this setup, is it true that every symmetry algebra of dimension $m$ may yield a cinf-structure in the same way that solvable symmetry algebras yield solvable structures?

Case n=3

Suppose a third order ODE with associated field $A$. Suppose we have a symmetry algebra of dimension 3 with basis $\{X,Y,Z\}$. Then

$$ \begin{align} [X,A]= \square A\\ [Y,A]= \square A\\ [Z,A]= \square A \end{align} $$

since they are Lie point symmetries.

In order to have a cinf-structure we only need that $[Y,X]\in \mathcal{S}(\{X,Y\})$, but

$$ [Y,X]=aX+bY+cZ. $$

We have that

$$ [Z,X]=a'X+b'Y+c'Z. $$

If we replace $Y$ by $\tilde{Y}:=Y-\frac{c}{c'}Z$ we have that

$$ \begin{align} [\tilde{Y},X]=[Y,X]-\frac{c}{c'}[Z,X]=\\ =aX+bY+cZ-a' c/c'X-b' c/c'Y -cZ=\\ =\square X+\square Y. \end{align} $$

But $\tilde{Y}$ still satisfies $[\tilde{Y},A]=\square A$, so $\langle X,\tilde{Y}, Z \rangle$ is a cinf-structure.
