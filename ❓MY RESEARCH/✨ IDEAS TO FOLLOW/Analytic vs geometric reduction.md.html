Analytic vs Geometric reduction

Slightly related: reduction of a distribution by a symmetry.

Consider the ODE $\Delta=u_2-\phi=0$, where $\phi$ is a smooth function defined on the jet bundle $J^2$. This ODE can be encoded with two different exterior differential systems:

(A). $(J^2, \mathcal{I}_2)$ where $\mathcal{I}_2=\{\theta_0,\theta_1,\Delta\}_{diff}=\{\theta_0,\theta_1,\Delta,d\theta_0,d\theta_1,d\Delta\}_{alg}$

(G). $(J^1, \mathcal{I}_1)$ where $\mathcal{I}_1=\{\theta_0,du_1-\phi dx\}_{diff}=\{\theta_0,du_1-\phi dx,d\theta_0,-d\phi \wedge dx\}_{alg}$

They are equivalent in the sense that an integral manifold of (A) gives rise to a unique integral manifold of (G) and reciprocally (proof!). Moreover, any such integral manifold corresponds in a 1-1 way to a solution of the ODE.

Now, let's consider a generalized Cinf-symmetry ODE $\bar{X}\in \mathfrak{X}(J^2)$ . We know it is the lambdaprolongation of a vector field $X\in \mathfrak{X}(J^1)$. Consider two invariants of $X$, $y$ and $w$, and construct the maps

$$ \bar{\pi}:J^2 \to J^1 $$

and

$$ \pi:J^1 \to J^0 $$

given by $\bar{\pi}=(y,w,w_1)$ and $\pi=(y,w)$.

The reduction process of @muriel01ima1 corresponds to take a section $\bar{\sigma}$ of $\bar{\pi}$ and define

$$ \tilde{\mathcal{I}}_2:=\bar{\sigma}^*(\mathcal{I}_2) $$

We have an exterior differential system $(J^1,\tilde{\mathcal{I}}_2)$ which represents the reduced equation.

\section{Alternative version: Geometric approach to ODE reduction}

\subsection{Classical procedure as a reduction "a la $\mathcal{J}^X$"}

The reduction process for ODEs once a Lie point symmetry or a generalized symmetry is known is a classical procedure that can be found in many references (\cite{olver86,Stephani} ...). More recently, a similar reduction procedure was introduced in \cite{muriel01ima1,MR2001541} by using \cinf-symmetries instead of Lie point symmetries. This common reduction procedure goes, in essence, as follows. Suppose we have an ODE like \eqref{eq_ode}, and let $X$ be a \cinf-symmetry in the sense of Definition \ref{def_lambdasym} (this include the case of Lie point symmetries and contact symmetries (?)). We can construct a complete set of invariants of $X$ once we know two of them, say $y$ and $w$ (see Remark \ref{rem_IBD}). Since $\Delta=u_m-\phi$ satisfies $X(\Delta)=0$ whenever $\Delta=0$, the function $\Delta$ can be written in terms of this complete set of invariants, and we have

$$ \Delta(x,u,\ldots,u_m)=u_m-\phi=\tilde{\Delta}(y,w,\ldots, w_{m-1})=\rho (w_{m-1}-\tilde{\phi}). $$

We call to the expression

  • \begin{equation}\label{eq_reducedODE1}

  • w_{m-1}=\tilde{\phi}(y,w_1,\ldots,w_{m-2})

  • \end{equation}

the reduced equation of the ODE \eqref{eq_ode} by means of $X$.

This "analytical" process can be understood in terms of exterior differential systems (\cite{bryant2013exterior}) in the following way. The ODE \eqref{eq_ode} can be encoded with the EDS $(J^m, \mathcal{J})$ where

$$ \mathcal{J}=\{\theta_0,\ldots,\theta_{m-1},\Delta\}_{diff}=\{\theta_0,\ldots,\theta_{m-1},\Delta,d\theta_0,\ldots,d\theta_{m-1},d\Delta\}_{alg}. $$

It is a mere computation to check that there is a 1-1 relationship between solutions of \eqref{eq_ode} and integral manifolds of $\mathcal{J}$.

Observe that $\mathcal{J}="\mathcal{C}\cup \{\Delta,d\Delta\}_{alg}"$ (maybe $\mathcal{J}=\mathcal{C}+ \{\Delta,d\Delta\}_{alg}$?), being $\mathcal{C}$ the contact ideal of $J^m$.

Given a vector field $X$ we can define the following set

$$ \mathcal{J}^X=\{\omega\in \mathcal{J}: X\contract \omega\in \mathcal{J}\}. $$

{\color{blue}

\begin{remark}

[For us, to be erased later] The vector field $X$ is trivially a Cauchy characteristic vector field of $\mathcal{J}^X$, in case $\mathcal{J}^X$ were an EDS. So $\mathcal{J}^X$ is, in a sense, the result of "appending a new vector field" to the "distribution" represented by $\mathcal{J}$. Of course, if $X$ were already a Cauchy characteristic vector field of $\mathcal{J}$ then $\mathcal{J}^X=\mathcal{J}$: we are doing nothing.

On the other hand, observe that in $\mathcal{J}$ there are 1-forms like $\alpha=3\Delta dx+u \Delta du$ such that $X\contract \alpha\neq 0$ but $X\contract \alpha\in \mathcal{J}$. These 1-forms are included in our $\mathcal{J}^X$ but are not considered by Fels (they require $X\contract \alpha= 0$). If the EDS doesn't have 0-forms I guess that both ideas are the same. But our apporach is better for two reasons:

\begin{enumerate}

  • \item It is more general, more elegant

  • \item It coincides with the definition of Cauchy characteristic vector field, so it fits better with Bryant...

\end{enumerate}

\end{remark}

}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem_condition_cinfsym}

If $X$ is a \cinf-symmetry in the sense of Definition \ref{def_lambdasym} then there exists a smooth function $f$ such that for every $\omega\in \mathcal{C}$

$$ X\contract d\omega +d(X\contract \omega)+\dfrac{df}{f}X\contract \omega \in \mathcal{C} $$

\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}

Since $X$ is a \cinf-symmetry, there exist $f$ such that $fX$ is a contact symmetry (it is deduced from \cite[Lemma 5.1]{muriel01ima1}). Then, for every $\omega\in \mathcal{C}$ we have $\mathcal{L}_{fX} \omega \in \mathcal{C}$. Now observe that

$$ \mathcal{L}_{fX} \omega=f\mathcal{L}_{X} \omega+df X\contract \omega $$

so

$$ \mathcal{L}_{X} \omega+\dfrac{df}{f} X\contract \omega\in \mathcal{C} $$

and hence

$$ X\contract d\omega +d(X\contract \omega)+\dfrac{df}{f}X\contract \omega \in \mathcal{C}. $$

\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}

If the vector field $X$ is a \cinf-symmetry of equation \eqref{eq_ode} then $\mathcal{J}^X$ is an EDS.

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}

The set $\mathcal{J}^X$ is an ideal, because given $\omega \in \mathcal{J}$ such that $X\contract \omega \in \mathcal{J}$ then given $\alpha \in \Omega^*(J^m)$ we have $\alpha \wedge \omega\in \mathcal{J}$ and

$$ X\contract \alpha \wedge \omega=X\contract \alpha \wedge \omega+(-1)^p \alpha \wedge (X\contract \omega) \in \mathcal{J}, $$

so $\alpha \wedge \omega \in \mathcal{J}^X$.

Now, we are going to see that if $X$ is a \cinf-symmetry, this ideal is differentially closed. Consider $\omega\in \mathcal{J}^X$, i.e., $\omega\in \mathcal{J}$ and $X\contract \omega\in \mathcal{J}$. We already know that $d\omega\in \mathcal{J}$, and we need to show that $X\contract d\omega \in \mathcal{J}$.

If $\omega\in \mathcal{C}$ then because of Lemma \ref{lem_condition_cinfsym} there exists a smooth function $f$ such that

$$ X\contract d\omega +d(X\contract \omega)+\dfrac{df}{f}X\contract \omega \in \mathcal{C}\subset \mathcal{J}. $$

We have that $X\contract \omega$ and $d(X\contract \omega)$ belong to $\mathcal{J}$, so $X\contract d\omega \in \mathcal{J}$.

If $\omega=d\Delta$, $d\omega=0$ so trivially $X\contract d\omega \in \mathcal{J}$.

Finally, if $\omega=\Delta$ then $X\contract d\omega =X\contract d\Delta=X(\Delta)$, which is 0 if $\Delta=0$. And therefore there exists a nonvanishing function $\rho$ (possibly we should shrink the domain of $\Delta$?) such that

$$ X\contract d\omega=\rho \Delta \in \mathcal{J}. $$

\end{proof}

{\color{blue}

\begin{remark}

[For us, to be erased later] This proposition could lead us to define a \textbf{reducing vector field} for an EDS $\mathcal{I}$ as a vector field $X$ such that $\mathcal{I}^X$ is again an EDS and $\mathcal{I}^X \neq \mathcal{I}$ (if not it will be a Cauchy characteristic vector field).

\end{remark}

}

It is known that \cinf-symmetries have the invariant-by-derivation property (Remark \ref{rem_IBD}), so we can construct a surjective submersion $\pi$.

Let's call $\tilde{\mathcal{J}}$ to the EDS of the reduced equation by the classical procedure. It would be interesting to prove that

$$ \tilde{\mathcal{J}}=\pi_*(\mathcal{J}^X) $$

and that

$$ \mathcal{J}^X=\pi^*(\tilde{\mathcal{J}}). $$

They are, indeed, equivalent (see exercise 8.35 and theorem 8.2 in Benjamin McKay Introduction to Exterior Differential Systems).
