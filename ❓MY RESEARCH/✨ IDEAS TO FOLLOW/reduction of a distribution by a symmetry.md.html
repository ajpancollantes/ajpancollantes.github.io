Reduction of a distribution by a symmetry

See maple_044 y videos youtube 000 y 001.

The idea is as follows. Given an involutive distribution $\mathcal{Z}=\{A_1,A_2,\ldots,A_r\}$ on an open set of $\mathbb{R}^n$ (with $n>r+1$), and a symmetry $X$ (probably it also works with cinf-symmetry of distribution) we can use it to pass to a distribution, still of rank $r$, but in a space of dimension $n-1$, preserving the information. The integral manifolds of the reduced distribution would let us obtain the integral manifolds of the original one.

How do we proceed? We use the invariants of $X$ to construct a projection onto $\mathbb{R}^{n-1}$. We could also add a variable to this projection in order to do pushforwards and pullbacks properly, if we want.

The vector fields $A_i$ , on their own, are not "projectable" but I think that the whole distribution $\mathcal{Z}$ is projectable, in the sense that although the "pushforwards" (abusing of language) of the vector fields do contain a "strange constant", they span the same distribution that a selected set of $r$ proper vector fields in $\mathbb{R}^{n-1}$ (without any "strange constant"). For the moment I only have a "empiric proof" (maple_044).

On the other hand, I think that if we express the distribution $\mathcal{Z}$ with $n-r$ differential forms $\omega_i$ such that the first $n-r-1$ contain the symmetry $X$ in their kernel, that is, they are of the form

$$ \omega_i:=\Omega(A_1,\ldots,A_r,X,\ldots,-),\quad i=1,\ldots,n-r-1 $$

then these 1-forms are "projectable" (not defined yet), that is, when we apply the "pullback" they have a factor that can be removed in order to obtain proper 1-forms in $\mathbb{R}^{n-1}$. Again, I have only empirical proof for the moment (this fact is formulated as a lemma, below). These projected 1-forms would describe the reduced distribution.

If we find the integral manifolds of these reduced 1-forms, we can use the "inverse" of the projection to obtain the integral manifolds of the distribution $\{A_1,\ldots,A_r,X\}$, in a way analogous to how we recover the solution of an ODE from the reduced ODE. At the end, we have to integrate one more 1-form, the contraction of $\{A_i\}$ with the volume form of the integral submanifold of $\{A_1,\ldots,A_r,X\}$ inside which we were. If $X$ is a symmetry this 1-form will be closed, and if $X$ is a cinf-symmetry it will be at least Frobenius integrable. Let's call this "the residual step".

The ideas we were discussing about hidden symmetries should work perfectly in this more general setup. If $X_2$ is a symmetry (cinf-symmetry) of $\mathcal{Z} \cup \{X\}$ such that $X_2$ is also symmetry of $X$ (that is, $X_2$ is projectable) then the projection of $X_2$ along $X$, $\tilde{X}_2$, is a symmetry of the reduced distribution. Observe that $X_2$ is not a symmetry of $\mathcal{Z}$. Reciprocally, given a symmetry $\tilde{X}_2$ of the reduced distribution (hidden symmetry), there must be a projectable vector field $X_2$, symmetry of $\mathcal{Z} \cup \{X\}$.

Additional considerations:

  • When $\mathcal{Z}=\{A\}$ is a second order ODEs and $X$ a symmetry, we are in the case of the classical Lie reduction, or lambdasymmetry reduction.

  • In the case of solvable structures (or cinf-structures) every step in the integration procedure is nothing but the residual step (see above).

  • On the other hand, I think that the "elegant proof" that Paola was looking for, for the transformation of the symmetry of distribution, $X_2$, into the hidden symmetry of the reduced equation, can be achieved if we have the "lemma" for "projectability" 1-forms (see below).

  • These ideas give more meaning to partial solvable structures, because now we could say that a partial solvable structure let us to reduce the "ambient space" of the equation. The problem is that if we use the last element $X_k$ of the partial solvable structure to reduce the distribution $\{...,A_i,\ldots,X_1,\ldots,X_{k-1}\}$ we don't have guarantee that this reduced distribution conserves "the internal structure". (I think we should require $X_k$ to be a cinf-symmetry of all the distributions: $\{\ldots,A_i,\ldots\}$, $\{\ldots,A_i,\ldots,X_1\}$, $\{\ldots,A_i,\ldots,X_1,X_2\}$, ..., and so on)

Lemma

Given an completely integrable Pfaffian system $\mathcal{P}=\{\omega_1,\ldots,\omega_{n-r}\}$ on a open set $U\subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ and a characteristic symmetry $X$ of $\mathcal{P}$ (i.e., $X\lrcorner \omega_i=0$ for every $i$), and being

$$ \pi:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^{n-1}, $$

a map consisting of the projection on the invariants of $X$, there exist $n-r-1$ independent 1-forms $\tilde{\omega}_i\in \Omega^1(\mathbb{R}^{n-1})$ such that

$$ \pi^*(\tilde{\omega}_i)=\sum \eta_{ij} \omega_j $$

with $\eta_{ij}$ smooth functions in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

$\blacksquare$

Proof

Frobenius theorem let us assure that there exist $n-r$ smooth functions

$$ F_i:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to \mathbb{R} $$

such that $\mathcal{P}=\{dF_1,\ldots,dF_{n-r}\}$, so there exist also smooth functions $\eta_{ij}$ such that $dF_i=\sum\limits_{j=1}^{n-r}\eta_{ij}\omega_j$ (at least in a reduced open set). According to the canonical form of a regular vector field, there exist a coordinate change

$$ \varphi:(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\mapsto(c,z_1,\ldots,z_{n-1}) $$

in such a way that $\varphi_*(X)=\partial_c$. Suppose without lose of generality that

$$ \pi:(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\mapsto(z_1,\ldots,z_{n-1}). $$

Since $X\,\lrcorner\, \omega_j=0$ for every $j$ we have $dF_i(X)=0$, so applying the coordinate change we obtain that $\partial_{c}(F_{i}\circ \varphi^{-1})=0.$ Therefore the 1-forms

$$ \tilde{\omega}_i:=(\varphi^{-1})^*(dF_i)= d(F_{i}\circ \varphi^{-1}) $$

can be projected to the $(z_1,\ldots,z_{n-1})$-space, and so $\pi^*(\tilde{\omega}_i)=\varphi^*(\tilde{\omega}_i)$. And so

$$ \pi^*(\tilde{\omega}_i)=dF_i=\sum\limits_{j=1}^{n-r}\eta_{ij}\omega_j $$

as required.

$\blacksquare$

Corollary

Given a Frobenius integrable 1-form $\omega$ on a open set $U\subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ and a vector field $X\in \mbox{Ker}(\omega)$ then, provided that $\pi:U\to \mathbb{R}^{n-1}$ consist of $n-1$ independent invariants of $X$, there exists a 1-form $\tilde{\omega}$ on $U'\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n-1}$ such that

$$ \pi^*(\tilde{\omega})=f\omega. $$

$\blacksquare$

That is: this 1-form can be projected.

On the other hand, I think that Corollary 2.3. in @bryant2013exterior is a particular case of this lemma.

Main result

Given an involutive distribution $\mathcal{Z}$ on an open set $U\subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ and a symmetry $X$ there exist:

  • an open set $\tilde{U}\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n-1}$.

  • an involutive distribution $\tilde{\mathcal{Z}}$ on $\tilde{U}$.

such that if the integral manifold of $\tilde{\mathcal{Z}}$ through $\varphi(p)\in \tilde{U}$ it is known then the integral manifold of $\mathcal{Z}$ through $p$ can be obtained by a quadrature.

Proof

Idea:

Construct the map $\varphi: U\to \tilde{U}$ with the invariants of $X$.

Construct $\tilde{\mathcal{Z}}$ with the lemma.

Once the integral manifold of $\tilde{\mathcal{Z}}$ through $\varphi(p)\in \tilde{U}$ is known, say $\tilde{N}$, then the integral manifold of $\mathcal{Z}$ through $p$ is contained in $\varphi^{-1}(\tilde{N})$. The 1-form not used for the projection (the one which doesn't contain $X$) should be the 1-form to integrate in order to get the solution.

$\blacksquare$

Variation: if instead of requiring $X$ to be a symmetry we require only to be a cinf-symmetry of distribution then the integral manifold is obtained by a Pfaffian equation.
