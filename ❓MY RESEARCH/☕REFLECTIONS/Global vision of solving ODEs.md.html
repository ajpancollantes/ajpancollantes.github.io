Global vision of solving ODEs

It is a particular case of alternative Lie method for first-order ODEs?

(Watch videos 014 1-2-3 "otro enfoque sobre las cinf-structures")

Given an $m$th-order ODE

$$\Delta\equiv u_m-\phi(x,u,\ldots,u_{m-1})=0$$

a very natural idea is to perform an order reduction by finding $F(x,u,\ldots,u_{m-1})$ such that

$$ D_x(F)=\mu \Delta $$

We call $F$ a first integral and $\mu$ an integrating factor. This way, for $C\in \mathbb{R}$ we could consider the ODE

$$ F(x,u,\ldots,u_{m-1})=C $$

of $(m-1)$th-order, and apply the same idea. At the end we eventually arrive to an expression depending on $x,u$ and $m$ constants. Of course, this is highly unlikely to happen.

On the other hand, the expression $u_m-\phi=0$ leads us, in a very naive way, to the 1-form $\omega_{\Delta}:=du_{m-1}-\phi dx=0$. It turns out that if we find $F$ such that

$$ dF=\mu \omega_{\Delta} $$

we will have obtained a valid pair $(F,\mu)$ to perform the reduction indicated above (the proof is a particular case of what is proven here).

It may happen that $\omega_{\Delta}$ is not integrable ($\omega_{\Delta}\wedge d\omega_{\Delta}\neq 0$). But maybe, if we "tweak" $\omega_{\Delta}$ a little by adding suitable multiples of the contact 1-forms of the Cartan distribution $\mathcal{E}$ (this corresponds to interchange $u_1 dx$ by $du$ when needed so $\omega_{\Delta}$ and $\widetilde{\omega}_{\Delta}$ are, from the point of view of Analysis, the same) we could arrive, with a bit of luck, to a 1-form $\widetilde{\omega}_{\Delta} \equiv \omega_{\Delta}$ mod $\mathcal{E}$ and such that is a Frobenius integrable 1-form, so we could find

$$ dF=\mu \widetilde{\omega}_{\Delta}. $$

It can be shown that also in this case $(F,\mu)$ is a valid pair for the reduction, and the proof is again here.

How can we find $\widetilde{\omega}_{\Delta}$?

Let fix $\Omega$ be the volume form. By duality in exterior algebras we know that a 1-form is associated, loosely speaking, with $m$ vector fields (remember that we are working in the jet bundle $J^{m-1}\equiv \mathbb{R}^{m+1}$). So we have to look for $m$ vector fields $X_i$, $i=0,\ldots,m-1$, such that

  • $\{X_0,X_1,\ldots,X_{m-1}\}$ are independent, in order to

$$ X_{m-1}\lrcorner \ldots X_1\lrcorner X_0\lrcorner\Omega=\Omega(X_0,X_1,\ldots,X_{m-1},-) $$

be a non-null 1-form.

  • $\{X_0,X_1,\ldots,X_{m-1}\}$ is involutive, in order to

$$ X_{m-1}\lrcorner \ldots X_1\lrcorner X_0\lrcorner\Omega $$

be Frobenius integrable (see dual charaterization involutiveness).

  • the vector field of the ODE, $A$, must be one of them (let's say $X_0=A$) in order to the 1-form $X_{m-1}\lrcorner \ldots X_1\lrcorner X_0\lrcorner\Omega$ be a linear combination of $\omega_{\Delta}$ and the contact 1-forms $\theta_i$ of the Cartan distribution $\mathcal{E}$.

So we have to look for $m-1$ vector fields satisfying (together with $A$) independence and involutiveness (just the conditions of cinf-structures!) . And from here we can make some considerations:

1. If we find a vector field that "complement" $A,X_1,\ldots,X_{m-1}$ and is a symmetry of $\mathcal{S}(A,X_1,\ldots,X_{m-1})$ the resulting 1-form is closed, so $\mu=1.$ This is in Basarab 1991, proposition 2 or [Sherring 1992] theorem 2.1.

2. If we require these conditions not only to $A,X_1,\ldots,X_{m-1}$ but to every $A,X_1,\ldots,X_{i}$ we arrive to the idea of cinf-structure. This "extra work" would let us chain the reductions and arrive (being very optimistic) to the general solution.

3. If we put together consideration 1 and consideration 2 we arrive to the idea of solvable structure.

Geometrical idea

Given $\Delta:J^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ we want to fin the solution curves contained in $S_{\Delta}=\{\Delta=0\}$. On the other hand, given any $F:J^{1}\subseteq J^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ we can consider the intersections

$$ \{\Delta(x,u,u_1,u_2)=0\}\bigcap\{F(x,u,u_1)=cte\}\subseteq J^2 $$

(we are abusing of language: indeed is $p^*(F)$ being $p$ the natural projection from $J^2$ to $J^1$).

These spaces are not, in general, interesting in order to solve the equation. But if they contained the solution curves I only would have to study the projection of the solution curves to $J^1$, contained in $\{F=cte\}\subseteq J^1$.

The fact is that they contain the solution curves if $A(F)=0$, which is equivalent to $dF\in \mbox{span}(\omega_{\Delta},\theta_0)$ (since $\mathcal{S}(A)^*=\mathcal{S}(\omega_{\Delta},\theta_0)$), equivalent to $D_x(F)=\mu\Delta$ (see here).

Symmetries and Cinf-symmetries of ODEs and distributions in this context

I think every method to solve ODEs can be understood from this point of view:

1. Of course, cinf-structures and solvable structures are included, as has been said above

2. Reduction with a Lie point symmetry of a first-order ODE is trivial

3. Reduction with a Lie point symmetry of a higher-order ODE. How does it help to find $\tilde{\omega}$? It doesn't do. It helps to find it in the final step.
