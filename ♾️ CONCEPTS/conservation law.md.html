Conservation law

Definition. @olver86 section 4.3.

Consider a system of DEs $\Delta_{\nu}(x,u^{(n)})=0$. A conservation law is an expression

$$ \mbox{Div} P=0 $$

which vanishes for all solutions $u=f(x)$ of the system. Here $P=(P_1(x,u^{(n)}),\ldots,P_p(x,u^{(n)}))$ is a $p$-tuple of smooth functions and $\mbox{Div}$ is the total divergence.

$\blacksquare$

In the particular case of a system of ODEs, where we have only 1 independent variable and $u$ represents functions

$$ u:x\mapsto (u^1,\ldots,u^q) $$a conservation law is of the form $D_x P=0$ for every solution of the system, that is, $P(x,u^{(n)})$ must be constant along solutions. Son in this case a conservation law is nothing but a first integral of the system.

In case our system of DE arise in a dynamical context, one of the independent variables is the time $t$ and the other $x_1,\ldots,x_p$ are spatial variables. The conservation law is given by

$$ D_t T+\mbox{Div}_{space} X=0 $$

The function $T$ is called the conserved density and $X=(X_1,\ldots,X_p)$ are called the associated flux. This is totally related to current density and charge density . Relate with Example 4.22 in @olver86.
