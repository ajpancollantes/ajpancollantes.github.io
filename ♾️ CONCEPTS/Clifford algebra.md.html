Clifford algebra

Is the same as Geometric Algebra, but from a different approach.

Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional real (complex) vector space with nondegenerate bilinear form $g$. The (complex) Clifford algebra $Cl(V,g)$ is the algebra generated by $V$ along with the anti-commutation relation

$$ xy+yx =2g(x,y) $$

If the signature of $g$ is $(p,q)$, then we use the notation

$$ Cl_{p,q} $$

or

$$ Cl(p,q). $$

Note: If $x,y\in V$, then $xy$ belong to the subset of $Cl(V,g)$ denoted by $V^2$.

Note: the Clifford algebra can be seen like coming from the tensor algebra.

Example: Pauli vectors constitute a representation of the Clifford algebra $Cl_{3,0}=Cl(3,0)$, since they satisfy

$$ \sigma_i \sigma_j+\sigma_j \sigma_i=2I\delta_{ij}. $$

It is called the Algebra of Physical Space (APS). Its matrix representation is given by the Pauli matrices. It is related to Pauli spinors

$\blacksquare$

Example: The Clifford algebra $Cl(1,3)$ is called the Spacetime Algebra (STA) and it is related to the Weyl spinors.

$\blacksquare$

3D Space $Cl(3,0)$, APS

  • Rotations in $ij$-plane:

$$ V \rightarrow e^{-\sigma_i \sigma_j \frac{\theta}{2}} V e^{\sigma_i \sigma_j \frac{\theta}{2}} $$

4D Spacetime $Cl(1,3)$, STA

  • Rotations in $ij$-plane:

$$ V \rightarrow e^{-\gamma_i \gamma_j \frac{\theta}{2}} V e^{\gamma_i \gamma_j \frac{\theta}{2}} $$

  • Boost in $k$-direction:

$$ V \rightarrow e^{-\gamma_t \gamma_j \frac{\theta}{2}} V e^{\gamma_t \gamma_j \frac{\theta}{2}} $$
