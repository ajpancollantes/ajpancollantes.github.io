Matrix algebra

The algebra made of square matrices is typically called the matrix algebra. More precisely, if $F$ is a field (such as the real numbers or the complex numbers) and $n$ is a positive integer, then the matrix algebra over $F$ of size $n\times n$ is denoted by $M(n, F)$ or $\mathcal{M}_n(F)$. It consists of all $n\times n$ matrices with entries in $F$, and the operations of addition and scalar multiplication are defined in the usual way.

It is a central algebra.

The action of the projective linear group $PGL(n, F)$ on the matrix algebra is given by conjugation. That is, for any $A$ in the matrix algebra and any $g$ in $PGL(n, F)$, the action of $g$ on $A$ is defined as $gAg^{-1}$, where $g^{-1}$ denotes the inverse of $g$. This action is well-defined since scalar matrices are in the center of $GL(n, K)$ and are therefore ignored in the quotient.

The automorphisms of this algebra are all inner automorphism, so they are all elements of$PGL(n,F)$, because two elements of $GL(n,F)$ give rise to the same inner automorphism if they are multiples of each other by a scalar matrix.
