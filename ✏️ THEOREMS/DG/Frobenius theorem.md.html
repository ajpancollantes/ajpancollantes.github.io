Frobenius theorem

A rank $r$ distribution $D$ on an $n$-dimensional manifold $M$ is involutive if and only if for every $p\in M$ there exists a coordinate chart $\left(U, \varphi=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\right)$ such that

$$ D=\mathcal{S}(\{\varphi_*^{-1}(\partial x_1),\ldots,\varphi_*^{-1}(\partial x_{r})\}) $$

(that is, is completely integrable distribution).

Equivalently, the dual description of the distribution would be

$$ D^*=\mathcal{S}(\{dx_{r+1},\ldots,dx_n\}) $$

Therefore, Frobenius theorem nos dice que las involutive distribution tienen neighbourhoods en los que existen integral submanifolds que pasan por cualquier punto dado con la mayor dimensión posible, y vienen dadas por

$$ N_{(c_{r+1},\ldots,c_n)}:=\{q\in U: x_i(q)=c_i \text{, for } i=r+1,\ldots,n\} $$

In infinite dimension, i.e., a Banach manifold, Frobenius theorem states that a subundle of the tangent bundle is integrable iff it is involutive. Ver este enlace en la sección Banach manifolds.

As a particular case we have the rank 1 distributions, that are always involutive and, therefore, always have integral manifolds called, in this case, integral lines. See canonical form of a regular vector field.
