Spectral theorem

In Linear Algebra and Analysis, a spectral theorem is a result about when a linear operator or matrix can be diagonalized (that is, represented as a diagonal matrix in some basis). This is extremely useful because computations involving a diagonalizable matrix can often be reduced to much simpler computations involving the corresponding diagonal matrix. The concept of diagonalization is relatively straightforward for operators on finite-dimensional vector spaces but requires some modification for operators on infinite-dimensional spaces.

An example of spectral theorem is the statement that every symmetric matrix is equivalent to a diagonal matrix via a orthogonal transformation:

$$ S=ODO^t $$

Another one is that every Hermitian matrix is unitarily equivalent to a diagonal one (that is, $H=UDU^{\dagger}$, with $U$ an unitary matrix and the dagger is the conjugate transpose).

The first example is a particular case of the latter.

The generalization of these statements to operators in Hilbert space is called spectral theory.
